--- title: "Heat transfer coefficient measurement of thick-walled inaccesible enclosure cooled internally using jet impingement (NC State University)" excerpt: "

Objective:

There are passive cooling systems designed to internally cool down small inaccesible and thick-walled enclosures like turbine blades and electronics packaging. The lack of accessibility and small size prohibit direct measurements to be done using conventional measurements.

Methodology

  • Analysed a simple unconfined jet impinging onto 1 surface of a sufficiently thick plate, but measured the opposite surface that was externally facing and exposed to the ambient. This was conceived as an easier precursor to a proper confined flow, by eliminating wall-bounded turbulence that would have complicated the heat transfer spatially.
  • This was achieved by creating a frame-like structure, and attaching the plate to 1 side, opposite side being an orifice through which air flows to create the impinging jet (image below).
  • Core problem statement below, simplifies to guessing the heat transfer coefficient on the inner surface that maps to the measured temperature of the external surface and considering through-thickness effects.
  • Ran transient experiments of jet-impingement heating, for 6 cases, of different flow rates and plate thicknesses.
  • Measured the ambient facing surface's temperature using infrared cameras, and tracked true temperatures on both surfaces using thermocouples for calibration
  • Conceptualised a non-linear-least-squares minimization problem on MATLAB, for the difference between the measured temperatures and the predicted temperatures on the external surface.
  • These predicted temperatures were generated from the solution to the problem's forward 3D heat conduction problem, assuming the unknown heat transfer coefficient produced by jet impiingement to be a parameterised function
  • Set-up conjugate heat transfer simulations on ANSYS Fluent for verification and validation.

Results

  • Obtained reliable estimates within sufficient error bounds (5%) from CFD results.
  • Spatial mean and stagnation came close to CFD predictions
  • Constraints applied to the optimization problem (bounding some values of the heat transfer coefficient to reliable pre-calculated estimates) helped the optimiztion process converge in a stable manner while preserving accuracy.
