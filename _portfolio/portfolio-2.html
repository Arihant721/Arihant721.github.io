Objective:

Jet impingement is highly effective in cooling localised hotspots, and is used in turbine blade leading edges since leading edges face the brunt of hot gases in the combustion chamber of a jet engine. To improve efficiency multiple jets in various layouts are used, which were investigated here.

Methodology

  • Worked under a PhD student, to measure the heat transfer coefficient on a simplified scaled-up turbine blade model made of acrylic
  • Impinged surface temperatures were measured using thermochromic liquid crystal coatings (this is a coating of special paint that changes color with change in temperature) and heat transfer coefficients were calculated using the semi-infinite heat conduction since acrylic is a good insulator.
  • Multiple layouts of holes, through which the coolant would flow and generate impinging jets, were considered here: centreline array, where the holes line up along a line and connect perpendicular to the edge circumference, and tangential, where the holes are shifted away, so that the flow is redirected along the curvature.
  • Ran transient experiments for 6 cases, for different flow rates and jet hole layouts.
  • Ran CFD simulations, for the full conjugate heat transfer problem of the jet impingement, and analysed the interactions between multiple impinging jet flows, and their effect on the heat transfer coefficient for the leading edge model.

Results

  • Tangential arrays showed higher efficiency than the centreline arrays.
  • CFD simulations predict a larger surface area under turbulent impinging flow as the reason for better cooling efficiency
  • Further modifications in arrays (staggered holes vs inline) also showed improvements.
